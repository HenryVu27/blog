Diagnosing RAG Failures

  • Every RAG failure is either a topic gap (the content doesn't exist in your corpus) or a capability gap (the system can't process what's there)
  • Take your last 20 failed queries, sort them into those two buckets -- you'll find patterns that point to specific fixes
  • Silent failures (corpus shrinkage from encoding bugs, stale embeddings, ingestion errors) are the deadliest because nothing looks broken
  • Evaluate what you didn't retrieve, not just what you did -- false negatives are harder to spot than bad answers

OpenClaw, the open-source AI agent that hit 300,000 users in three months, explicitly rejects RAG. Its creator's position is clear: "RAG is for looking up information, but OpenClaw needs a brain." Memory lives as plain Markdown files on disk. No vector database, no embedding pipeline, no retrieval stage. The agent reads what it wrote and writes what it learns. Simple, transparent, and effective enough to make OpenClaw the most popular personal AI agent of 2025.

Then you look at OpenClaw's plugin ecosystem, where the real memory work happens. The LanceDB memory plugin chunks Markdown into ~400-token segments with 80-token overlap, embeds them, and runs hybrid vector + BM25 search with MMR deduplication. The SQLite memory backend implements the same pattern: cosine similarity for semantic recall, FTS5 for exact-match keywords, weighted fusion at 70/30. Peel back one layer and you find the exact retrieval pipeline this post describes, wearing a different name.

That's the thing about RAG engineering: you can reject the label, but if your system needs to find relevant information from a growing corpus, you end up reinventing the same patterns. Part 1 showed a one-line tool call that did hybrid retrieval with condition-aware filtering. This post is about what's inside that call, and more importantly, how to tell when it's broken. I'm starting with diagnosis rather than architecture, because if you can't tell why your system is failing, you can't tell which fix will actually help. A better embedding model won't fix a corpus that's missing content. A fancier reranker won't help if your chunks were garbled during ingestion. Retrieval quality is the ceiling on everything downstream, and it fails quietly: a bad generation is obviously wrong, but a bad retrieval just looks like a plausible answer built on the wrong evidence.

Topics vs. Capabilities

The framing that makes RAG debugging click comes from Jason Liu: sort every failure into one of two buckets. A topic gap means the content doesn't exist in your corpus. No retrieval engineering can find a document that was never indexed. A capability gap means the content exists but the system can't surface it: chunking splits a critical table across fragments, the embedding model doesn't understand domain abbreviations, or the query is too vague for semantic search to latch onto.

The fixes are completely different. Topic gaps are content problems; you solve them by expanding the corpus. Capability gaps are engineering problems; you solve them with better chunking, retrieval strategies, or query understanding. Don't spend weeks fine-tuning embedding models when the real issue is that nobody ingested the product docs from the last three releases.

Case Study: Construction Blueprint QA

A construction company's RAG assistant handled questions about building blueprints. When they sorted their failure logs, roughly 20% of all queries were some variant of "how many windows are on the north elevation?" or "count the fire exits on floor 3." Topic gap disguised as a capability gap. The team assumed retrieval was failing, but the corpus contained blueprint PDFs with no extracted count data. The blueprints had the visual information; the text extraction pipeline just didn't produce anything a text-based retriever could match.

They added a vision model to the ingestion pipeline that extracted object counts and spatial relationships from blueprint images, then indexed those as structured annotations. User satisfaction went from ~50% to 87% in one week. Not because the retrieval got smarter, but because the content finally existed in a retrievable form.

RAG debugging is lumpy. You don't usually have 50 failure modes each at 2%. You have two or three dominant patterns that explain most of the bad retrievals. Find those and you get outsized returns.

Silent Failures

The scariest RAG failures are the ones where everything appears to work. A medical chatbot's ingestion pipeline processed documents as UTF-8, but a subset of the corpus (older clinical reference PDFs) used Latin-1 encoding. No errors. The system silently garbled special characters during text extraction, degrading embedding quality enough that those chunks vanished from retrieval results. A routine audit found 21% of the corpus had been functionally invisible since launch. Users got confidently wrong answers built from the remaining 79%, and nobody noticed because the system never returned empty results.

This is what makes RAG evaluation harder than generation evaluation. A hallucination is visibly wrong. A bad retrieval looks like a plausible answer built on the wrong evidence. You have to evaluate what didn't show up, which requires knowing what should have, and most teams don't have that ground truth. (I'll cover how to build it in Section 6.)

Applying the Framework

In my diabetes coaching agent, the two-bucket framing made failures legible immediately. The most common class was topic gaps: users asking about insulin pump troubleshooting (recalibrating after a site change, CGM readings diverging from finger sticks by 20%+), and the corpus simply didn't have pump-specific articles. It had plenty of general Type 2 content, so the retriever dutifully returned those documents, and the model generated answers that were technically about diabetes but useless for someone wearing an Omnipod.

The capability gap showed up in drug interaction queries. "Can I take metformin with lisinopril?" needs cross-referencing medication data with contraindication information. Both halves existed in the corpus, but they lived in separate collections with different schemas, and the retrieval layer had no mechanism for joining across them. The content was there; the system just couldn't compose it. That's a capability gap, and the fix is better retrieval architecture, not more content (Section 3).

Try this: pull your last 20 failed queries (thumbs-down ratings, cases where the model hedged with "I don't have enough information") and sort them into topic gaps vs. capability gaps. One bucket will be much larger. Within each bucket, two or three patterns will explain most failures. That's your roadmap. Do it again in two weeks, because the distribution shifts as your corpus and user base evolve.

Query Understanding

  • Structured extraction -- parse user questions into typed fields (date ranges, filters, keywords) before hitting any search backend
  • Query transformation techniques (HyDE, decomposition, RAG-Fusion) can boost retrieval accuracy 8-10% and comprehensiveness 30-40%
  • Better queries beat better embeddings almost every time -- a well-structured query with a mediocre embedding model will outperform a raw query with a state-of-the-art one
  • Adds 500-700ms latency per query, but enables temporal reasoning, filter routing, and multi-backend dispatch that you can't get any other way

The single highest-ROI improvement you can make to a RAG system isn't a better embedding model or a fancier reranker. It's spending an LLM call to understand the query before sending it to retrieval. The baseline pattern (embed the raw question, nearest-neighbor lookup, done) breaks in predictable ways: semantic gaps between how users phrase questions and how documents are written, no temporal reasoning ("recent guidelines" has no meaning in embedding space), and missing user context ("what should I eat?" means completely different things for a Type 1 patient on an insulin pump versus someone managing prediabetes through diet).

QUERY_REWRITE_PROMPT = """You are a search query optimizer for a coaching knowledge base.

Given a user's current message and recent conversation context, rewrite the query
to be self-contained and optimized for retrieval. Resolve pronouns, add implicit
context, and focus on the core information need.

Current query:
<query>{query}</query>

Recent conversation:
<conversation>{conversation_history}</conversation>

Patient profile: {family_profile}

Rules:
- Output ONLY the rewritten query, nothing else
- Keep it concise (under 30 words)
- Resolve pronouns (e.g., "he" -> the patient's name or "my patient")
- Add relevant context from the conversation (e.g., condition type, challenges)
- Strip emotional language — focus on the information need
- If the query is already self-contained, return it unchanged

Examples:
- Query: "What do I do when it won't go down?" | Context: discussing post-meal spikes,
  Type 2 -> "strategies for Type 2 diabetes post-meal blood sugar spikes not responding"
- Query: "I'm SO frustrated, nothing works" | Context: bedtime insulin timing
  -> "bedtime insulin timing strategies not working alternatives Type 2 diabetes"
- Query: "Does exercise help with that?" | Context: discussing A1c reduction
  -> "exercise impact on A1c reduction Type 2 diabetes evidence"

That prompt is the actual query rewriter from the diabetes coaching agent. The three slots ({query}, {conversation_history}, {family_profile}) give the LLM enough to resolve ambiguity. The examples do the heavy lifting, tuned to the failure modes that kept recurring: pronoun-heavy follow-ups, emotionally charged messages where the information need was buried, and short queries assuming context from earlier in the conversation. In Part 1, I showed the search_knowledge_base tool with its filter parameters. What I didn't show was this rewriting step that runs before that tool fires.

class QueryRewriter:
    async def rewrite(
        self,
        query: str,
        conversation_history: list[dict] | None = None,
        family_profile: dict | None = None,
    ) -> str:
        if not conversation_history:
            return query  # Nothing to resolve — pass through

        # Extract last 3 turns for context (enough to resolve pronouns,
        # not so many that the rewriter gets confused)
        recent_turns = conversation_history[-3:]
        history_lines = []
        for turn in recent_turns:
            role = turn.get("role")
            content = turn.get("content", "")
            if role == "user":
                history_lines.append(f"Patient: {content}")
            elif role == "assistant":
                history_lines.append(f"Coach: {content}")

        # Build profile context from what we know about the patient
        profile_text = ""
        if family_profile:
            parts = []
            if family_profile.get("condition_type"):
                parts.append(f"Condition: {family_profile['condition_type']}")
            if family_profile.get("challenge_areas"):
                parts.append(f"Challenges: {', '.join(family_profile['challenge_areas'])}")
            profile_text = "; ".join(parts)

        prompt = QUERY_REWRITE_PROMPT.format(
            query=query,
            conversation_history="\n".join(history_lines),
            family_profile=profile_text or "Not yet gathered",
        )

        rewritten = await self._llm.generate(prompt, temperature=0.0)
        rewritten = rewritten.strip().strip('"').strip("'")

        # Sanity check: if the rewrite is empty or suspiciously long, fall back
        if rewritten and len(rewritten) < 500:
            return rewritten
        return query  # Graceful fallback to original

Conversation-context rewriting matters more than keyword-based routing because most retrieval failures come from follow-up questions, not initial queries. A user's first message is usually specific enough ("what's the recommended A1c target for elderly patients?"). It's the third or fourth message that breaks things: "what about if I'm also on insulin?" and the retriever has no idea what "also" refers to. The rewriter resolves those references by pulling in the last three turns. temperature=0.0 for deterministic rewrites, and the fallback-to-original pattern means a rewriter failure never blocks the pipeline.

Query Transformation Techniques

Beyond structured extraction, four query transformation techniques each solve a different retrieval failure mode.

HyDE (Hypothetical Document Embeddings) generates a hypothetical answer to the user's question, then embeds that instead of the question. Even if the answer is wrong, it captures the vocabulary of a real answer better than a question does, because questions and documents occupy different regions of embedding space. Helps most when queries are short or abstract ("managing fatigue") and the corpus contains detailed clinical prose. Doesn't help when the query is already specific and technical.

Query decomposition handles multi-hop questions. "How does metformin interact with the ketogenic diet for someone with kidney concerns?" is three sub-questions packed into one. No single chunk answers all three. Decompose, retrieve for each, assemble a more complete answer. A simple heuristic works: queries containing two or more distinct medical concepts get automatically decomposed.

Step-back prompting works the opposite direction: instead of more specific, more conceptual. "Why did my blood sugar spike to 280 after eating white rice?" becomes a question about glycemic index and carbohydrate metabolism. Retrieves broader, foundational documents. Most useful for "why" questions where the user needs understanding, not facts.

RAG-Fusion is the brute-force option and honestly the most consistently effective. Generate 3-5 query variations via LLM, retrieve separately for each, fuse with Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF). Rackauckas (2024) reports +8-10% accuracy and +30-40% on comprehensiveness. Cap at 5 sub-queries: beyond that, results drift off-topic and latency scales linearly.

Shifting Work from Runtime to Ingestion

You can shift this cost to ingestion time. Run an LLM over each chunk to generate the questions it would answer, then embed the question-answer pairs. At runtime, user questions get compared against other questions in embedding space rather than document prose: a much tighter semantic match. HyDE in reverse, but you pay the compute once during indexing rather than on every request.

The shared tradeoff: query understanding adds 500-700ms of latency. But it unlocks temporal reasoning (filtering by publication date), filter routing (medication queries to drug references, nutrition queries to recipes), and multi-backend dispatch (keyword search for drug names, semantic search for conceptual questions). For every production RAG system I've worked on, the 600ms has been worth it.

Building the Retrieval Pipeline

  • Chunking -- start with 400-512 tokens, 10-20% overlap; Anthropic's contextual chunking (prepend LLM-generated context per chunk) cuts retrieval failures by 49%, 67% with reranking on top; embedding models silently truncate oversized inputs, so chunking matters even with 200K context windows
  • Hybrid retrieval -- combine dense embeddings + sparse BM25 via Reciprocal Rank Fusion; 18% improvement over dense-only, and BM25 is 10x faster on keyword-heavy queries
  • Reranking -- cross-encoder rerankers add +33-40% accuracy for ~120ms latency; over-fetch 20-50 candidates, rerank down to 5-10
  • Keep everything in one database (full-text search + vector embeddings + SQL filters) rather than maintaining separate indices per collection
  • Put your most relevant chunks first and last in the context window -- the lost-in-the-middle effect shapes ordering strategy

With query understanding handling intent, we can turn to the pipeline itself: chunk the corpus, retrieve candidates via hybrid search, rerank with cross-encoders, and order the final set for the context window. Most of the gains come from getting these fundamentals right, not from chasing exotic techniques.

Chunking

Start boring. A RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter at 400-512 tokens with 10-20% overlap will get you surprisingly far. I spent a week building a custom markdown-aware splitter before discovering that the recursive character splitter performed within 2% on retrieval recall. One thing to watch: embedding models silently truncate inputs that exceed their max sequence length. Hand a 512-token model a 900-token chunk and it embeds the first 512 tokens, ignores the rest, raises no error. This is why chunking matters even with 200K context windows: the embedding model's limit (512-8192 tokens) constrains retrieval quality, not the generation model's.

Once basics are solid and you've identified chunking as a genuine bottleneck through Section 1's diagnostic framework, three techniques are worth investing in.

Anthropic's contextual chunking was the biggest single improvement I found. For each chunk, an LLM generates 50-100 tokens of context situating it within its source document, prepended before embedding. A chunk reading "Reduce dosage by 25% if eGFR falls below 30 mL/min" becomes "This excerpt is from the ADA 2024 clinical guidelines on metformin use in patients with CKD. Reduce dosage by 25% if eGFR falls below 30 mL/min." Anthropic's benchmarks: contextual embeddings alone cut retrieval failures by 35%, combined with contextual BM25 by 49%, add reranking and you're at 67%. Cost: $1.02 per million document tokens with prompt caching.

Late chunking (Jina AI) processes the entire document through the transformer first, letting self-attention propagate context, then splits the final-layer embeddings into chunks. Each chunk inherits document-wide context through attention rather than prepended text. 3.5% relative gain on nDCG, scaling with document length. Cleaner architecturally, but requires long-sequence embedding models.

Proposition-based chunking decomposes documents into atomic factual claims. 17-25% improvement on Recall@5 in benchmarks, but a strong caveat: on clinical decision support tasks it was among the worst performers. Fragmenting guidelines into atomic propositions strips away qualifications and dosage ranges. "Metformin should be discontinued" is technically self-contained, but without "when eGFR drops below 30 mL/min in patients with Stage 4 CKD," it's dangerous. Highly domain-dependent: good for factoid QA, bad for anything where context carries clinical or legal weight.

I settled on contextual chunking with a 400-token base size. The prefix adds 50-80 tokens per chunk (within the embedding model's 512-token budget), and retrieval recall jumped from 72% to 84% Recall@10 on our test set. Medication chunks that previously lacked disease context now matched properly against condition-specific queries.

Hybrid Retrieval

Dense vector search alone leaves gaps that are embarrassing once you notice them. Search for "HbA1c" using embeddings trained on general text and cosine similarity scores are mediocre, because the model hasn't seen enough endocrinology literature to place that abbreviation near "glycated hemoglobin." BM25 handles this effortlessly. The reverse is equally common: "blood sugar management strategies" won't keyword-match "glycemic control interventions," but dense search bridges the gap.

Hybrid retrieval runs both in parallel, then fuses results. I've measured an 18% improvement in Recall@20 over dense-only, consistent with what others report. BM25 has a speed advantage too: roughly 10x faster on keyword-heavy queries (inverted index lookups vs. nearest-neighbor search). On queries with exact medical terms, BM25 returns in under 5ms while vector search is still computing distances.

Reciprocal Rank Fusion (RRF)

Given retrieval results from multiple rankers r, the fused score for document d is:

$$\text{Score}(d) = \sum_{r} \frac{1}{k + \text{rank}(r, d)},$$

where k = 60 (the standard constant from the original Cormack et al. paper).

Why this works: RRF ignores the raw scores from each retriever entirely -- it only cares about rank positions. This is the key property, because you don't need to normalize or calibrate scoring systems that may operate on completely different scales (BM25 scores in the range 0-25, cosine similarity in 0-1). A document ranked #1 by one system and #3 by another gets a higher fused score than a document ranked #2 by both. The formula is parameter-free beyond the constant k, which controls how much weight top-ranked results get relative to lower-ranked ones. In practice, k = 60 works well across domains without tuning.

One decision that saved real operational pain: keep everything in a single database. PostgreSQL with pgvector gives you full-text search (BM25 via tsvector), vector similarity, and SQL filtering in one system. A single query finds chunks similar to X, filtered by condition_type and date, ranked by BM25: no orchestration across services. The separate-index-per-collection pattern creates overhead that isn't justified unless your scale genuinely demands it.

Reranking and Authority

Retrieval gets you candidates; reranking sorts them by actual relevance. Over-fetch 20-50 candidates, then pass each (query, chunk) pair through a cross-encoder that scores joint relevance. Cross-encoders are more accurate than bi-encoders because they process query and document jointly through the transformer, at the cost of linear scaling with candidate count. Across benchmarks: +33-40% retrieval accuracy for ~120ms of additional latency. The best accuracy-per-millisecond trade in the entire pipeline. After reranking, take the top 5-10 and discard the rest.

class FastEmbedReranker:
    """Cross-encoder reranker using FastEmbed's ONNX runtime.
    Runs locally — no API calls, no per-query cost."""

    def __init__(self, model_name: str = "BAAI/bge-reranker-base"):
        from fastembed.rerank.cross_encoder import TextCrossEncoder
        self._model = TextCrossEncoder(model_name=model_name)

    async def rerank(
        self,
        query: str,
        results: list[RetrievalResult],
        top_k: int,
    ) -> list[RetrievalResult]:
        documents = [r.content for r in results]

        # Run CPU-bound ONNX inference off the event loop
        raw_scores = await asyncio.to_thread(
            lambda: list(self._model.rerank(query, documents))
        )

        # Raw logits → sigmoid → calibrated [0, 1] probabilities
        scores = [1 / (1 + math.exp(-s)) for s in raw_scores]

        scored = sorted(zip(scores, results), key=lambda x: x[0], reverse=True)
        for ce_score, result in scored[:top_k]:
            result.score = ce_score  # Replace RRF score with calibrated reranker score

        return [result for _, result in scored[:top_k]]

This runs locally via ONNX: no API calls, no per-query cost. The sigmoid normalization matters because the model outputs raw logits, not probabilities. After sigmoid, 0.7 consistently means "probably relevant" regardless of query or candidate count. Without it, you'd be setting thresholds in logit-space, which is unintuitive and unstable. The asyncio.to_thread wrapper keeps ONNX inference from blocking the event loop when reranking 30+ candidates per request.

Reranking fixes ordering, but semantic similarity and authority are not the same thing. Search for "how to fix a Python import error" and a casual Reddit comment will often score higher than the official docs, because it's linguistically closer to how the query is phrased. Cosine similarity rewards resemblance, not expertise. In medical contexts, this is dangerous.

The full solution is learning-to-rank (LTR): train a model to combine BM25 score, cosine similarity, domain authority, freshness, and engagement into a final relevance score (Perplexity does this). For the coaching agent, I went simpler: tiered authority weights on RRF scores. ADA guidelines get 1.5x, peer-reviewed journals 1.2x, curated content 1.0x, community-sourced 0.7x. Crude, but an ADA guideline about insulin titration now always outranks a forum post on the same topic.

Context Window Ordering

One more decision affects answer quality more than you'd expect: chunk ordering in the context window. The lost-in-the-middle effect (Part 1) shows up to 73% degradation when critical information sits in the middle. Put your most relevant chunks first and last, supporting chunks in the middle. Anthropic found that including the top 20 chunks outperformed top 5 or 10 (more context helps reasoning), but only if you order it so the best pieces aren't buried.

Production Patterns Worth Stealing

MMR (Maximal Marginal Relevance) solves the redundancy problem you'll hit once retrieval works well enough to return 10+ relevant chunks. Four of your top results might say nearly the same thing, pulled from adjacent sections of the same guideline. MMR iteratively selects results balancing relevance against redundancy: MMR = λ × relevance − (1−λ) × max_similarity_to_already_selected. With λ=0.7, you get diversity without sacrificing much relevance. WeKnoRa (Tencent's production RAG framework) uses Jaccard similarity over tokenized passages for the redundancy term, fast enough for every query. This eliminated the "five paraphrases of the same ADA guideline" problem that was wasting 40% of my context window.

Composite scoring blends signals instead of replacing the base score with the reranker score: 0.6 × reranker + 0.3 × base_retrieval + 0.1 × source_weight. The base score captures retrieval-stage signals (BM25 overlap, embedding proximity) that cross-encoders might miss on short chunks. Source weight encodes domain priors. WeKnoRa adds a position prior favoring chunks from document beginnings (clamped to ±5%), which tracks with how technical documents front-load key claims.

Threshold degradation handles the edge case where your reranker filters everything. Instead of returning empty, retry at 70% of the original threshold (floor of 0.3). # Qdrant hybrid search: dense + sparse with server-side RRF fusion
def search_hybrid(
    self,
    query_vector: list[float],
    query_text: str,
    top_k: int = 10,
    filters: RetrievalFilters | None = None,
) -> list[tuple[int, float, dict]]:

    qdrant_filter = self._build_filter(filters) if filters else None
    sparse_vec = self._text_to_sparse(query_text)
    prefetch_limit = min(top_k * 3, len(self.chunks))

    results = self._client.query_points(
        collection_name=self._collection,
        prefetch=[
            Prefetch(query=query_vector, using="dense", limit=prefetch_limit),
            Prefetch(query=sparse_vec, using="sparse", limit=prefetch_limit),
        ],
        query=FusionQuery(fusion=Fusion.RRF),  # Server-side RRF — no manual merging
        query_filter=qdrant_filter,
        limit=top_k,
    ).points

    return [(point.id, point.score, point.payload) for point in results]

Qdrant handles RRF fusion internally through the Prefetch + FusionQuery pattern. Define prefetch stages for dense and sparse, and the database merges server-side. No hand-rolled RRF, no score normalization. The prefetch_limit of top_k * 3 over-fetches so fusion has enough material; the filter applies before fusion so you only fuse over documents matching your metadata constraints.

TAG_BOOST = 0.15

class HybridRetriever:
    async def retrieve(
        self,
        query: str,
        top_k: int | None = None,
        filters: RetrievalFilters | None = None,
        state: SessionState | None = None,
    ) -> RetrievalResponse:
        top_k = top_k or settings.RAG_TOP_K

        # Step 1: Query rewriting (conversation context → self-contained query)
        search_query = query
        if self._rewriter and state and state.conversation_history:
            rewritten = await self._rewriter.rewrite(
                query=query,
                conversation_history=state.conversation_history,
                family_profile=state.family_profile.model_dump() if state.family_profile else None,
            )
            if rewritten != query:
                search_query = rewritten

        # Step 2: Hybrid search — over-fetch if reranker is available
        fetch_k = settings.RAG_RERANK_CANDIDATES if self._reranker else top_k
        candidates = await self._hybrid_search(search_query, fetch_k, filters)

        # Step 3: Cross-encoder reranking (optional, behind config flag)
        if self._reranker and len(candidates) > top_k:
            candidates = await self._reranker.rerank(search_query, candidates, top_k)

        # Step 3b: Relevance threshold — only with reranker
        # (RRF scores aren't calibrated, so thresholding them is meaningless)
        if self._reranker:
            candidates = [r for r in candidates if r.score >= settings.RAG_RELEVANCE_THRESHOLD]

        # Step 4: Compute facets for follow-up filtering
        facets = self._compute_facets(candidates)

        return RetrievalResponse(results=candidates[:top_k], facets=facets)

The key decision: fetch_k. If a reranker is configured, over-fetch (typically 3-5x top_k) so the cross-encoder has material to reorder. No reranker? Just fetch top_k directly. The relevance threshold only applies with reranking, because RRF scores are rank-based and vary across queries in ways that make a fixed threshold meaningless. After reranking, scores are sigmoid-normalized logits, so 0.4 consistently means "at least moderately relevant."

Tag boosting is a post-retrieval score adjustment: if query keywords match a chunk's tags, bump the score by 0.15 per match. Cheap authority signal, not a reranker replacement. The facet computation aggregates metadata across results (document types, tags, sources), powering follow-up filtering: "I found 3 clinical guidelines and 2 patient education articles. Want me to focus on the guidelines?"

When Graphs Beat Vectors

  • Most teams don't need GraphRAG -- vector RAG with hybrid search handles simple factual lookups well enough, and graph indexing costs roughly 10x more than vector indexing
  • Scale compounds the pain: entity resolution alone hits ~15% error rates, and one team (Particula, 12M nodes) spent 3 months reaching 99.3% accuracy on entity deduplication
  • Where graphs genuinely win: multi-hop reasoning (87% accuracy vs 23% for traditional RAG), corpus-wide thematic queries, and entity-relationship traversal across documents
  • Budget-friendly alternatives exist -- LazyGraphRAG cuts query cost by 700x, KET-RAG reduces indexing cost by 82%, and PathRAG beats full GraphRAG in 60% of head-to-head comparisons

If your retrieval pipeline from Section 3 is working (hybrid search, reranking, contextual chunking), you're handling the vast majority of production queries. Don't reach for a graph until you've hit a specific wall that vectors can't solve, because the engineering cost is significantly higher than the demos suggest.

The Scale Problem

Start with money: vectorizing The Wizard of Oz costs ~$0.006. Building a knowledge graph from the same text (entity extraction, relationship classification, community detection) costs ~$0.06 with GPT-4o-mini. That's 10x for a children's book. A 1,000-page PDF runs ~$120 for full graph construction at GPT-4-Turbo rates, before you've spent a dollar on query-time inference.

But the dollar cost isn't the biggest problem. Entity resolution ("Dr. Smith," "J. Smith, MD," "Smith (2024)," and "the lead researcher" are the same person) hits ~15% error rates. Errors compound: duplicated entities spawn incorrect relationships and degrade every downstream operation. Particula (12M-node graph) spent three months and 2,400 manually verified training matches to reach 99.3% accuracy on entity dedup, and that's a team with Neo4j expertise.

Query execution adds more pain. LLM-generated Cypher is only ~77% correct: one in four queries returns wrong results or fails. One production team abandoned it entirely for 30 manually maintained query templates (96%+ accuracy). And graph explosion means innocuous queries ("find all connections between Company X and pharmaceuticals") can touch hundreds of thousands of nodes, requiring traversal budgets and result caps (Particula used 500-node limits) that mean you're seeing a sample, not the full picture.

When Graphs Genuinely Win

Multi-hop reasoning is the clearest win. "Which drugs interact with the medications prescribed to patients who also have condition X?" needs connecting information across 3+ documents. Benchmarks show 87% accuracy for graph-based approaches vs. 23% for traditional RAG. Corpus-wide thematic queries ("What are the main themes across Q4 customer complaints?") require aggregating patterns across the entire corpus: GraphRAG's Leiden clustering pre-computes hierarchical community summaries that flat retrieval can't produce. Entity-relationship traversal ("Which suppliers share warehouses with our top 3 customers?") needs walking specific paths through connected nodes. And temporal reasoning across document versions needs time-stamped entity connections that embedding similarity can't capture.

If you're trying to decide whether your use case justifies the investment, this is the framework I'd use:

Cheaper Alternatives

The cost calculus changes every few months. LazyGraphRAG defers entity extraction to query time, indexing at ~0.1% of full GraphRAG's cost and querying at ~4% (roughly 700x cheaper). KET-RAG uses PageRank to skip low-degree leaf nodes that rarely participate in useful queries: 18.3% of indexing cost with superior coverage (81.6% vs. 74.6-79.6%). PathRAG hit a 60% win rate against full GraphRAG by optimizing traversal rather than construction. HippoRAG reports 20% improvement on multi-hop tasks at 10-30x lower cost.

I don't use graph retrieval in the diabetes agent. The queries are single-hop factual lookups and hybrid search handles them. But if you're building a system that needs to connect treatment protocols across conditions, link drug interactions to trial outcomes, and traverse patient-condition-medication paths, that's a graph problem. Diagnose first. Reach for graphs only when your diagnosis points specifically at multi-hop or relationship-traversal failures.

Self-Correcting Retrieval

  • Traditional RAG is fire-and-forget -- retrieve once, generate, hope for the best; next-gen systems evaluate retrieval quality during generation and correct course when it's poor
  • CRAG routes through three corrective paths (correct / incorrect / ambiguous) that refine, replace, or supplement retrievals; +19 points on PopQA when integrated with Self-RAG
  • Self-RAG trains four reflection tokens (Retrieve, ISREL, ISSUP, ISUSE) that let the model decide when to retrieve, evaluate relevance, and check whether generation is actually supported by evidence
  • RAG paradoxically reduces a model's ability to abstain -- more context means more confidence, which means more hallucination when that context is insufficient; sufficiency checking before generation improves correct answers by 2-10%

Everything so far is fire-and-forget: retrieve once, generate, hope. If retrieval was bad, generation will be bad, and you won't know until a user complains. The systems below evaluate retrieval quality mid-generation and correct course when it's poor.

CRAG: Corrective Retrieval-Augmented Generation

Corrective RAG (Yan et al., 2024) puts a lightweight evaluator between retriever and generator, routing to three paths. Correct: refine documents by decomposing chunks into knowledge strips and recomposing only what's relevant. Incorrect: discard everything and fall back to web search (this handles the topic gaps from Section 1). Ambiguous: refine and supplement with web search. CRAG alone gives +3.6 on PopQA; stacked with Self-RAG, +19 on PopQA and +36.6 on PubHealth. The key: it's plug-and-play on top of any existing pipeline.

Self-RAG

Self-RAG (Asai et al., 2023) trains the model to emit reflection tokens that control retrieval: Retrieve ("should I search?"), ISREL ("is this passage relevant?"), ISSUP ("is my generation supported by evidence?"), ISUSE ("is this useful?"). Retrieval becomes a decision the model makes mid-generation, not a one-time event. A 7B Self-RAG model shows ~40% improvement over RAG baselines. The catch: requires fine-tuning with reflection tokens, so you can't bolt it onto Claude or GPT-4 via API.

Search-R1 (Jin et al., 2025) pushes self-correcting retrieval further by training search behavior directly through reinforcement learning. The model learns when and what to search during its chain-of-thought reasoning, issuing search queries as actions within the reasoning trace and incorporating results before continuing. The key technical innovation is retrieved token masking -- when computing the RL loss, the gradient is applied only over tokens the LLM itself generated, excluding all retrieved content from gradient updates. This prevents the model from being rewarded or penalized for the quality of search results it can't control, focusing the learning signal purely on the model's search decisions and reasoning. With Qwen2.5-7B as the base model, Search-R1 achieves 41% improvement over RAG baselines on multi-hop question answering benchmarks. The approach requires RL training infrastructure (they use GRPO, a variant of PPO), but it points toward a future where retrieval isn't a pipeline stage bolted onto generation but a learned capability integrated into the reasoning process itself.

FLARE

Forward-Looking Active REtrieval (Jiang et al., 2023) is the most pragmatic option. No training, no special tokens: monitor token-level confidence during generation, and if any token falls below a probability threshold, trigger retrieval using the tentative sentence as query, then regenerate with new context. You only pay retrieval latency when the model actually needs help. Needs logprobs access (available from most APIs now) and a per-domain confidence threshold.

RAG Makes Abstention Worse

Google Research (2024) found that RAG makes models worse at knowing when they don't know. Without retrieval, unfamiliar questions get hedged. Add a retrieval step (even irrelevant context) and confidence spikes: the model interprets document presence as evidence. Their fix: a sufficiency check before generation, a lightweight classifier evaluating whether retrieved context actually answers the query. This single gate improved correct answers by 2-10%, not by improving retrieval but by preventing generation when retrieval had failed. If you're building anything where a wrong answer is worse than no answer (medical dosing, legal advice, financial guidance), add a sufficiency check.

What's Production-Ready

CRAG you can build in a weekend: evaluator + routing on any existing pipeline, no retraining. Start here. FLARE is my pick for moving beyond fire-and-forget: logprobs + confidence threshold + re-query. Pairs naturally with the sufficiency check. Self-RAG has the best benchmarks but requires fine-tuning. Search-R1 is a research preview using RL-trained retrieval; not production-ready yet, but the retrieved token masking trick will show up in production within a year or two.

The RAG Flywheel

  • Q-C-A framework: every RAG system has a Question, Context, and Answer -- 6 conditional relationships between these three components cover all the evaluation you need
  • The 9-step RAG flywheel takes you from synthetic data generation through production monitoring to continuous iteration -- the loop matters more than any individual step
  • About 90% of teams adding sophisticated retrieval or reranking techniques performed worse when properly evaluated against baselines
  • Build your own eval with ~300 labeled chunks, iterated 4-5 times; MTEB leaderboard rankings don't predict performance on your domain
  • Focus on leading metrics (weekly experiments conducted, eval suite execution time) over lagging metrics (overall quality scores that move too slowly to be actionable)

I've spent the last five sections showing you techniques that improve retrieval quality. Here's the uncomfortable truth: most teams that add these techniques without proper evaluation make their systems worse, not better. The missing piece isn't a fancier retriever. It's knowing whether what you built actually works.

Q-C-A: Six Metrics That Cover Everything

Every RAG system has three components that matter for evaluation: a Question (what the user asked), a Context (what the retriever returned), and an Answer (what the model generated). Between those three, there are six conditional relationships, and every RAG metric you've ever encountered maps onto one of them.

The Q-C-A Evaluation Framework

TierMetricWhat it measuresWhen to use
FoundationPrecision & Recall @KRetrieval accuracyDaily, no LLM needed
PrimaryContext Relevance (C|Q)Do retrieved chunks address the question?Every eval run
PrimaryFaithfulness (A|C)Does the answer stay within context? (hallucination)Every eval run
PrimaryAnswer Relevance (A|Q)Does the answer address what was asked?Every eval run
AdvancedContext Support (C|A)Does context contain everything the answer needs?Deep analysis
AdvancedAnswerability (Q|C)Can the question be answered from this context?Deep analysis

Run the three Primary metrics every evaluation cycle. Context Relevance (C|Q): did retrieval pull documents that address the question? This is the most fundamental metric, because if retrieval fails, everything downstream fails. Faithfulness (A|C): is the answer supported by the retrieved context? Answer Relevance (A|Q): does the output address what was asked? Start with the Foundation tier (Precision/Recall @K) because you can compute it without LLM calls: labeled queries paired with correct chunks, check if they show up. Fast, deterministic, tight feedback loop.

Choosing the Right @K

Different K values tell you different things. Recall@5 is what your users experience. Recall@25 tests your reranker (right chunk in top 25 but not top 5 = reranker bottleneck). Recall@50 checks your embedding model. Recall@100 is an existence check (not there = not in corpus or badly chunked).

Watch for Simpson's Paradox: your overall Recall@5 might look great at 85%, but factoid questions are 98% and multi-hop questions are 40%. The aggregate hides that you're failing on the queries that matter most. Always segment by query type, topic, and complexity.

The operational process that ties evaluation to continuous improvement is the RAG flywheel -- nine steps that cycle from initial baseline through synthetic data, real-world feedback, and targeted fixes:

  1. Initial implementation -- build a baseline system, however simple. You need something running to measure against.
  2. Synthetic data generation -- for each chunk in your corpus, generate plausible questions that chunk should answer. This gives you a labeled evaluation set without manual annotation, and typically achieves ~97% baseline accuracy (since the questions are derived directly from the chunks).
  3. Fast evaluations -- run precision and recall against your synthetic dataset. No LLM calls needed, so this executes in seconds. Speed is the point; if your eval takes 30 minutes, you won't run it after every change.
  4. Real-world data collection -- log production queries. The gap between synthetic questions and what users actually ask is where most of the real improvement opportunity lives.
  5. Classification and analysis -- cluster your real queries by topic, intent type, and capability requirement. This is where the topic-gap vs. capability-gap framework from Section 1 becomes operational.
  6. Targeted improvements -- fix failures by category, not globally. If medical dosage queries are failing, improve the medical content and chunking strategy, don't retune your entire embedding model.
  7. Production monitoring -- track your Q-C-A metrics continuously, with alerts on regressions. A change that improves one query category can silently degrade another.
  8. User feedback integration -- collect structured feedback (more on what "structured" means in a moment).
  9. Iterate -- go back to step 2. The synthetic dataset grows, the real-world dataset grows, and each cycle through the loop improves your understanding of where the system fails.

Step 3 is where most teams fail. If your eval takes 20 minutes, you'll run it once a day. If it takes 15 seconds, you'll run it after every change. Build the fast path first (precision/recall on synthetic data, no LLM calls), layer on LLM-judge metrics for weekly deep dives.

90% of Improvements Make Things Worse

~90% of teams adding sophisticated retrieval techniques performed worse when properly evaluated against baselines. Not "didn't help": actively worse. This number comes from Jason Liu and Skylar Payne. Teams add hybrid search, swap in a reranker, implement query expansion, and the complexity introduces regressions that outweigh gains. A reranker that improves factoid queries by 10% but degrades multi-hop by 25% looks great on cherry-picked examples and terrible across a real eval set. The takeaway: measure before and after, across all query categories, or you're probably making things worse.

Leading vs. Lagging Metrics

Stop obsessing over lagging metrics (aggregate quality scores, overall satisfaction). Track leading metrics instead: how many retrieval experiments did you run this week? Is your eval suite under 30 seconds? How many new labeled query-chunk pairs did you add? A team running 10 experiments a week with fast evaluation will outperform one running one experiment a month with a beautiful dashboard.

Fixing Feedback

Thumbs up/down and 5-star ratings are useless: a thumbs-down conflates bad retrieval, hallucination, wrong tone, slow response, and ugly formatting. Replace it with one question: "Did we answer your question correctly? Yes or no." This isolates correctness from presentation. Add an optional "what was wrong?" freeform. Teams I've talked to cut feedback analysis time by 60-70% after this switch.

Why MTEB Misleads

Don't pick your embedding model from the MTEB leaderboard. Weights & Biases found their top-ranked MTEB model underperformed alternatives on domain-specific tasks. Build your own benchmark: ~300 labeled query-chunk pairs from your domain, test candidate models on Recall@5 and Recall@25, iterate 4-5 times adding production failure cases. 300 well-curated domain pairs beat 10,000 academic benchmark results for model selection.

Automated evaluation frameworks: RAGAS and BenchmarkQED both provide implementations of the Q-C-A metrics described above, with LLM-as-judge scoring and synthetic test generation. They're useful starting points, especially RAGAS's faithfulness and answer relevance scorers. But I'd still prioritize building your own domain-specific eval suite over relying on a generic framework -- the framework tells you how to measure, but only your labeled data tells you what to measure against. Use the frameworks for the LLM-judge plumbing; supply your own ground truth.

When Not to Use RAG

  • Grep beats embeddings for code: Augment reached the top of SWE-Bench Verified using grep and find, not embedding-based retrieval -- agent persistence compensates for simple tools
  • In agent systems, the model becomes the orchestrator choosing which retrieval tools to invoke, not a passive consumer of a fixed pipeline
  • RAG is shifting from QA (save time) to report generation (inform decisions) -- a $20K system informing a $5M decision is cheap; a free system saving 10 minutes is nearly worthless
  • Embeddings remain essential for unstructured corpora, fuzzy conceptual queries, and non-text media where keyword search genuinely can't reach

Augment Code reached the top of SWE-Bench Verified using grep and find as their primary retrieval tools. Not a vector database, not a hybrid pipeline. Plain text search over a codebase, driven by an agent that formulated good queries and retried when results were bad. Source code is highly structured: function names, class hierarchies, and import paths act as natural keywords, and grep -rn "handleTimeout" gets you there faster than embedding similarity. Nik Pash (who built Cline) abandoned RAG entirely for coding agents. Colin Flaherty at Augment uses this test: "If a really persistent human that never got tired would this tool help? If yes, it's useful for the agent." For structured code, the answer kept coming back no.

This isn't just a coding-agent quirk. Tencent's WeKnoRa framework ships both knowledge_search (semantic) and grep_chunks (literal keyword) as parallel retrieval options. The agent chooses: proper nouns and error codes go to grep, conceptual questions go to embeddings. The deeper lesson is control inversion. In traditional RAG, retrieval is a fixed pipeline stage designed by an engineer. In agent systems, the model decides which tools to invoke, in what order, with what parameters. Simple, composable tools (grep, file read, SQL) become surprisingly effective when the model can chain them and retry on failure.

Embeddings remain essential where keyword search can't reach: unstructured corpora (Slack, wikis, email) with inconsistent vocabulary, content outside the model's training data, non-text media needing cross-modal retrieval, and fuzzy conceptual queries where the user doesn't know the right keywords. If your corpus is messy and your queries are vague, you still need the full pipeline. The question is whether to default to it, or start simple and add complexity when measurement demands it.

RAG is moving from question-answering to report generation, and the economics flip entirely. RAG-as-QA saves 5-10 minutes; the ceiling on that value is low. RAG-as-report-generation synthesizes hundreds of documents into coherent analysis, enabling decisions a human analyst couldn't make at the same coverage. A $20K/month system informing a $5M investment decision is cheap. A free chatbot saving 10 minutes on HR questions is nearly worthless.

Which brings us back to where we started. OpenClaw's creators called RAG a "lookup tool" and built memory as plain files instead. Then the community built plugins implementing the exact retrieval pipeline this post describes: chunking, hybrid search, reranking, MMR. You can reject the label, but if your system needs to find relevant information from a growing corpus, you end up reinventing the patterns. The patterns are real because the problem is real. What changes is how you wire them together: as a fixed pipeline, as composable agent tools, or as file-first memory with retrieval underneath. The engineering underneath stays the same.

Retrieval handles external knowledge: documents, databases, APIs. But what about knowledge the system has learned from this specific user over weeks of interaction? Preferences, past decisions, patterns that worked. That's memory, and memory engineering is where context persistence gets genuinely hard. Part 3 will go there.
